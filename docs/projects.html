DREAMS Malawi: Dietary Diversity Analysis

Quantitative Analysis

January 2026 – April 2026  |  Chiradzulu, Phalombe, and Thyolo districts, Malawi

After an El Niño-related drought worsened food insecurity in southern Malawi, CARE's DREAMS program combined cash assistance, agricultural support, nutrition services, and protection programming to help vulnerable families. I led the analysis of one core question: did increased access to nutrient-rich foods translate into measurable improvements in what women and young children were actually eating?

Using DREAMS household survey data from 2024 (baseline) and 2026 (endline) across three drought-affected districts, I ran a repeated cross-sectional analysis with adjusted prevalence ratio models, controlling for district, respondent age, education, marital status, and household size. The two primary outcomes were the Minimum Dietary Diversity for Women (MDD-W) and the Child Minimum Dietary Diversity (MDD) for children 6–23 months.

Key finding: Dietary diversity improved meaningfully across all three districts. Women at endline were roughly twice as likely to meet MDD-W (adjusted PR 2.01, 95% CI 1.67–2.41), and children were about 3.5 times as likely to meet child MDD (adjusted PR 3.51, 95% CI 2.56–4.81). Because the study had no control group, results are interpreted as associations rather than causal effects.

Skills used: R, prevalence ratio modeling, survey data analysis, repeated cross-sectional design, scientific poster design, evidence synthesis.

DREAMS Malawi Baseline: Interactive Hunger & Diet Diversity Dashboard

Data Visualization

Spring 2026  |  R flexdashboard with Plotly & Leaflet

Building on the DREAMS capstone, I designed an interactive dashboard to make the baseline survey data accessible to program staff and non-technical decision-makers. The dashboard visualizes household hunger and dietary diversity for 661 households surveyed across Chiradzulu, Phalombe, and Thyolo districts in October 2024, helping users quickly see where hunger is most severe and whether it tracks with poorer diet quality.

The dashboard answers two questions. First, where is hunger most severe? A district-level Leaflet map paired with a ranked dot plot compares three hunger indicators (no food in household, went to bed hungry, and went without food day and night) across districts. Second, is diet diversity lower in households reporting hunger? An interactive Plotly box plot compares food-group consumption between households that did and did not report someone going to bed hungry, with a dropdown to switch between women of reproductive age and children 6–23 months.

Why it matters: Targeting food security interventions in resource-limited settings depends on knowing where need is concentrated and which households are most at risk. By making the baseline data interactive and explorable, the dashboard turns a static dataset into a tool program staff can actually use to prioritize action.

Skills used: R, flexdashboard, Plotly, Leaflet, data wrangling with dplyr, dashboard design, communicating quantitative findings to non-technical audiences.
